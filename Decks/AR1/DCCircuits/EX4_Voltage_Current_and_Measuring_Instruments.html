Voltage

What is Voltage?

Electricity is the flow of electrons through a conductor. To cause that flow, a force must be applied to the electrons. That force is called the electromotive force (EMF), or more commonly, voltage.

Voltage indicates the intensity of the force exerted on the electrons: the higher the voltage, the stronger the push, and the higher the resulting current.

Unit volts (V) — named after Italian physicist Alessandro Volta Symbol E (also written as V or U)
Voltage

Potential Difference

Voltage is always measured as a difference in charge (potential difference) between two points in a circuit.

When two points are connected by a conductor, current flows from the point with the highest voltage to the point with the lowest voltage.

Example: A 1.2 V battery has its positive terminal at 1.2 V and its negative terminal at 0 V. The potential difference across both terminals is 1.2 V.
Current

What is Current?

While voltage is the force pushing electrons, current is the number of electrons passing through a conductor per second. The higher the current, the greater the flow of electricity.

Current is named after French mathematician and physicist André-Marie Ampère, considered the father of electrodynamics.

Unit amperes (A) Symbol I
Current

Direction of Current Flow

(a) Electron flow convention
Current flows from the negative terminal to the positive terminal — the actual direction electrons move. Rarely used in practice.
(b) Conventional current
Current flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal. This is the standard used in this course and most engineering contexts.

The Water Tank Analogy

A filled tank has high water pressure and produces high water flow. A depleted tank has low pressure and low flow.

Voltage & Current

Charged vs. Discharged Battery

The more electric charge in the battery, the higher the voltage across its terminals and the higher the current flowing through the load. A high electrical charge results in high voltage and high current.

A voltmeter is designed so virtually no current flows through it, preventing measurement errors.

Current Measurement

Using an Ammeter

—
Connect the ammeter in series — break the circuit and insert the ammeter so all current flows through it.
+
Connect the red (+) probe so current enters through it, and the black (COM) probe so current exits.
Warning: An ammeter has near-zero resistance — a very high current can flow if improperly connected. Always observe polarity.

Multimeter

Introduction to the Multimeter

A multimeter combines multiple measuring instruments in one device. Most measure DC and AC voltages, DC and AC currents, and resistance.

The measurement function is selected with a rotating knob. DC and AC modes are identified by their symbols:

DC
AC

Functions labeled in red on the multimeter are not yet covered in this course.

Multimeter

Before Turning the Power On

Always verify the following before making a measurement:

  • The multimeter is properly connected for the parameter being measured — in parallel for voltage, in series for current.
  • The red (+) probe is connected to the correct terminal for the measurement function (e.g., the current terminal when measuring current).
  • Probes are connected with respect to polarity — reversing polarity inverts the sign of the reading.
  • The correct function is selected for the parameter being measured.
  • The value to measure is within the range of the selected function (e.g., use the V setting, not mV, for a 24 V measurement).
Training System Component

The DC Motor

The DC motor in the training system converts electrical energy into kinetic energy, making the rotor (rotating part) spin.

Applying a different voltage changes its rotation speed rather than stopping operation. At 24 V, it rotates at 50 r/min.

Exercise 4: Summary

Key Takeaways

Voltage (E)

The force that pushes electrons. Measured in volts (V) with a voltmeter connected in parallel.

Current (I)

The rate of electron flow. Measured in amperes (A) with an ammeter connected in series.

Multimeter

Combines voltmeter, ammeter, and ohmmeter. Always verify connection, polarity, function, and range before use.
